What Is Gastric Sleeve Revision?

A gastric sleeve revision is a procedure to modify a previous sleeve gastrectomy. It typically involves converting it into another type of bariatric surgery, such as SIPS or gastric bypass. VSG revision is often aimed at patients who have experienced changes in their gastric sleeve size, requiring an adjustment to the original surgery for continued weight loss success.

Am I a Candidate for Gastric Sleeve Revision?

Ideal candidates for VSG revision are patients who initially experienced significant weight loss with a sleeve gastrectomy but have since regained weight or those who have not succeeded in reaching their goals. This is often due to changes in the size of the gastric pouch. A thorough evaluation by a healthcare provider, including reviewing your weight loss history and possibly conducting gastrointestinal X-rays, is essential to confirm if a gastric sleeve revision is the right step. Ultimately, this procedure is aimed at individuals seeking to reinvigorate their weight loss journey after the initial gastric sleeve surgery. Additionally, gastric sleeve revision can also be considered for those who have experienced complications or issues from their initial surgery, aiming to rectify problems and improve overall outcomes.

Reasons Gastric Sleeve Revision Is Needed

VSG revision may be necessary due to the following:

  • Gastric Sleeve Dilation or Stretching: Natural adaptation or overeating can stretch the sleeve , diminishing the surgery's effectiveness.
  • Inappropriate Weight Loss: Both insufficient and excessive weight loss can make gastric sleeve revision necessary.
  • Diet Non-Adherence: Failing to follow the strict post-surgery diet can lead to overeating and pouch stretching.
  • Reflux and Heartburn: Increased acid reflux or heartburn post-sleeve gastrectomy can necessitate VSG revision.
  • Complications from Initial Surgery: Issues like leaks or strictures from the first surgery might require corrective gastric sleeve revision.

Benefits of Gastric Sleeve Surgery

VSG surgery has become the most commonly performed weight loss procedure primarily because it has the lowest long-term risk. In many cases, it can be done on an outpatient basis, with as little as a week or two off work. Here are some of the advantages associated with this procedure:

  • Effective Weight Loss: Gastric sleeve surgery has a proven track record of enabling substantial weight loss. By reducing the stomach's size, it naturally curtails food intake, aiding individuals in shedding a significant percentage of their excess body weight.
  • Lowered Appetite: Gastric sleeve surgery also impacts the hormones related to hunger and satiety, which can lead to a reduction in appetite and a natural regulation of food intake.
  • Improved Comorbid Conditions: The weight loss resulting from VSG surgery often leads to the improvement or resolution of obesity-related conditions. Many studies show up to an 80% remission rate for Type II diabetes!
  • Absence of Foreign Objects: The short-term risk is slightly higher than with Lap Band; however, the long-term risk is much lower as there are no foreign bodies introduced during surgery.
  • Higher Self-Esteem: The positive change in body image and weight loss often contribute to enhanced self-esteem and a more positive outlook on life.
  • Minimal Maintenance: Unlike other bariatric procedures, gastric sleeve surgery requires minimal long-term maintenance and has no foreign objects like bands or ports that need adjustment.
  • Insurance Coverage: Many private insurance companies cover the costs associated with VSG surgery, making it a financially viable option for those seeking a surgical solution to weight loss.

What Are the Risks of Gastric Sleeve Surgery?

Like any surgical procedure, gastric sleeve surgery carries certain risks, though it is considered quite safe, especially when performed by experienced surgeons like those at JourneyLite. Here are some potential risks:

  • Surgical Complications: As with any surgical procedure, there are risks with VSG surgery, such as infection, bleeding and adverse reactions to anesthesia.
  • Leaks: There's a risk of leaks occurring along the staple line used to form the gastric sleeve, although such incidents are uncommon in VSG surgery.
  • Nutritional Deficiencies: Since gastric sleeve surgery alters the size of the stomach, there may be a risk of nutritional deficiencies post-surgery if the patient doesn't eat a balanced diet. Proper food choices and nutritional supplements can help prevent this.
  • Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD): Some patients may experience increased GERD symptoms after VSG surgery because the anatomical alterations caused by removing a portion of the stomach may disrupt the normal functioning of the lower esophageal sphincter.
  • Stomach Stretching: Over time, the remaining portion of the stomach may stretch due to overeating, potentially reducing the effectiveness of the surgery in terms of weight loss. If weight regain occurs, VSG surgery can easily be converted to a gastric bypass or SIPS, likely leading to additional weight loss.
  • Newer Procedure: Unlike the more established gastric bypass surgery, gastric sleeve surgery has only been an option for about 15 years, making it less time-tested.
  • Non-Adjustable and Non-Reversible: Unlike the Lap Band, gastric sleeve surgery does not allow for adjustments or reversals post-operation.

It's essential to have a thorough discussion with your healthcare provider to understand the risks and benefits of VSG surgery and to determine whether it's the right choice for your weight loss goals. VSG Surgery vs. Lap Band

What are VSG surgery differences compared to using a Lap band?

  • Since there is no mechanical device, there are fewer food intolerances with vertical sleeves than with the Lap Band.
  • Weight loss is much faster with gastric sleeve surgery than with the Lap Band.
  • Removing the portion of the stomach that secretes hunger hormones, such as ghrelin, can result in better hunger reduction than with the Lap Band.
  • There is no implantable device, so things like band slips and erosions arenâ€™t a concern down the road with the VSG surgery.
  • The risk is lower than with gastric bypass, yet the weight loss is similar.
  • Since there is no device to adjust, the follow-up regimen for VSG surgery is less intense than for the Lap Band.
  • VSG surgery is also a good revision procedure for band patients who have failed to lose weight or had to have their band removed.
